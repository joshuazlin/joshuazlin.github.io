Hamlet

Random number: 13

Summary:

Hamlet is just like me, having gone to another country to study in University. He's having the time of his life, being philosophical, when suddenly he's called back to Denmark to attend his father's funeral. And he finds out his Uncle has married his Mother within a month of his father's death.

Hamlet's Father visits the castle of a ghost however, and calls Hamlet out to speak to him. The ghost reveals that Claudius (Hamlet's Uncle) had murdered him, and tasks Hamlet with getting revenge on Claudius. Hamlet swears that he will, but decides not to tell anybody about what the ghost says, instead feigning madness for the rest of the play.

At the same time, the King and Queen are getting more and more worried about Hamlet's declining mental health. At the request of the King and Queen, Rosencrantz and Guildenstern return to Denmark to talk to Hamlet to try and find out the source of his sadness, though Hamlet suspects that they have been tasked by the Queen, and is saddened by their betrayal.

Polonius, one of the King's men, tells the King that perhaps Hamlet is being tortured by the fact that Polonius' daughter, Ophelia, had spurned his advances, and suggests staging a meeting between Hamlet and Ophelia to find out if this is the cause of Hamlet's madness. Hamlet confronts Ophelia, but is maddened by her scornful words, and harshly rebukes Ophelia.

Hamlet recruits the help of a few visiting players to help catch the conscience of the King. Hamlet suggests that if the players were to re-enact the killing of Hamlet's Father, perhaps the King's conscience will recoil, and the audience shall see a visible reaction in the King's countenance. Indeed, the King reacts violently, and runs from the scene to seek light.

Hamlet is told by Guildenstern that he needs to speak with his mother in her bedchamber. He agrees, but stops by the chapel on the way, in which the King is confessing the murder to God. Hamlet, however, doesn't kill the King, saying that killing the King while he is praying would send his soul straight to heaven, which isn't what he wants.

Hamlet continues to his mother's chamber, and harshly accosts her of infidelity to his father. Just as he was being violent, the Queen calls for help, and it is revealed that Polonius has been hiding behind a curtain, spying on the two. Hamlet kills Polonius, and drags his body of the bedchamber.

The King plans on sending Hamlet to England with a letter suggesting that Hamlet be executed the moment he steps off the ship. Hamlet, not knowing about this murderous plan, agrees however to leave Denmark, and leaves.

Laertes returns to Denmark, enraged that his father Polonius has been killed. At the same time, Ophelia goes mad with grief over the loss of both Hamlet and her father, and eventually drowns herself. Hamlet's ship is waylaid by pirates, and Hamlet makes an escape, editing the letter and sending Rosencrantz and Guildenstern to their deaths. Hamlet is now on his way back to meet Horatio.

Hamlet and Laertes meet at Ophelia's funeral ceremony, and, both enraged, enter a fight. It is suggested that they temporarily cease their brawl, however, and commence it with a swordfight at a later time. Claudius gives Laertes a poisoned sword, and also prepares a chalice of poisoned wine, to make sure that Hamlet is killed at this opportunity. However, during the swordfight, both Laertes and Hamlet are wounded by the poisoned sword, and the Queen drinks the poisoned wine. In his dying moments, Laertes reveals that the King was the conspirator all along, and Hamlet slays the King, dying with him.

Analysis:
The ghost has come back to task Hamlet with taking revenge on Claudius. What's interesting about this scene is how it is staged; in reality the ghost would be someone under the floorboards, running to and fro shouting, there could be room for metatheatricality when Hamlet says "Here and Everywhere?"

With an auspicious and a dropping eye,

Analysis:
UNSURE Falsity of emotion is normally revealed in the play by an obvious artificiality of language, whether it be Osric's compliments or Laertes' rant at Ophelia's death, or Claudius' over-fluency which can produce such grotesque images as 'with an auspicious and a dropping eye.
